# Dermatology — Clinical Reference Knowledge

## Skin Temperature Mapping
- **Normal skin temperature**: 33.2–36.9°C (varies by body region and ambient conditions)
- **Core-to-skin gradient**: Normal 2–4°C. Gradient >4°C may indicate vasoconstriction or shock
- **Bilateral asymmetry**: >1°C difference between corresponding regions → investigate vascular or inflammatory cause
- **Fever pattern on skin**: Generalized elevation >37.5°C at skin surface correlates with systemic fever

## Temperature Zone Norms (DS18B20 Sensor Array)
| Zone | Normal Range | Clinical Significance |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| Axilla | 36.0–37.0°C | Standard proxy for core temp |
| Cervical (C7) | 36.2–37.2°C | Sensitive to systemic infection |
| Forehead | 35.5–36.5°C | Convenient but environmentally influenced |
| Chest | 36.0–37.0°C | Stable, good for trending |
| Extremities | 30.0–35.0°C | Highly variable, perfusion-dependent |

## Diaphoresis (Excessive Sweating) Assessment
- **Generalized**: Consider hypoglycemia, MI, shock, hyperthyroidism, menopause
- **Localized**: May indicate nerve damage, compensatory hyperhidrosis
- **Cold/Clammy skin + diaphoresis**: Cardiogenic shock, severe hemorrhage
- **Night sweats**: Evaluate for TB, lymphoma, endocarditis, medications

## Thermal Anomaly Classification
- **Hyperthermia**: Skin temp >38.0°C. Grades: mild (38–39°C), moderate (39–40°C), severe (>40°C)
- **Hypothermia**: Core <35°C. Mild (32–35°C), Moderate (28–32°C), Severe (<28°C)
- **Localized hot spot**: Consider cellulitis, abscess, DVT, fracture, inflammatory arthritis
- **Localized cold spot**: Consider arterial insufficiency, Raynaud's, compartment syndrome

## Skin Lesion Classification (ABCDE Rule — melanoma screening)
- **A**symmetry, **B**order irregularity, **C**olor variation, **D**iameter >6mm, **E**volving

## Red Flags Requiring Immediate Escalation
- Rapidly spreading erythema with fever → necrotizing fasciitis
- Generalized urticaria + hypotension → anaphylaxis
- Core temperature >40°C or <32°C
- Purpura/petechiae + fever → meningococcemia
